This brief and somewhat racy hymn has attracted a great deal of scholarly atten tion. Most scholarly treatments are more or less in agreement with GeldnerтАЩs interpretation, signaled already in his title тАЬDie vereinfachte SomapressungтАЭ (The Simplified Soma-Pressing). In this interpretation the hymn depicts an abbreviated soma-pressing done at home, on the spur of the moment and without formality, by a sacrificer and his wife, using normal household implements, the mortar (ul┼л╠Бkhala) and pestle, to pound the soma stalks. The participation of the wife occasioned the slightly salacious comparisons between the pounding and sexual intercourse. A woman, presumably the wife, is mentioned explicitly once, in a word play that implicitly compares her regular movements in wielding the mortar with rhythmic sexual movement (vs. 3). Despite the weight of scholarly opinion, we consider this literal interpretation quite unlikely, because the precious and prized substance soma, the focus of the most elaborate non-royal rites, can hardly have been used for a slapdash, playful domestic entertainment. Instead, this hymn should be considered together with a number of others in the late R╠еgveda in which women figure in controversial or con
tested roles (e.g., I.179, X.10, X.102, X.109). In our view (see Jamison 2011, forth coming a, forthcoming b, in addition to background in 1996a), the late R╠еgveda saw the introduction of a new ritual model, in which the Wife of the Sacrificer partici pated in the ritual along with her husband and the other male functionaries. The word m├бnth─Б- occurs only here in the RV; it clearly refers to the churning stick later known as mantha or c─Бt(t)ra-, which is bound around with cords that, manipulated by a priest, move it rapidly back and forth while its bottom tip is inserted in the hole (yoni) of the lower kindling stick (ar├бс╣Зi-) to create the friction that produces fire. 4a): though acc., the reins (ra┼Ыm─л╠Бn) should not be directly compared to the churning stick, also acc. (manth─Б╠Бm), and we might rather expect instr. *ra┼Ыm├нbhiс╕е.
